The Films of Billy Wilder

A beginners guide to one of Holywoods best greatest directors: his life, genres, and his best films.

Who is Billy Wilder?

Billy Wilder (1906 - 2002) was an Austrian-American filmaker widley reguarded as on the of most versatile and accomplished directors in film history. His career spanned over five decades. Born in Austria-Hungry to a Jewish family. Moving around Europe in his early years, he fled the content for the United States with the rise of Nazism and antisemitism in Germany. In the 1930's he arrived in Hollywood with very little money and could only make out a few words in English, but he still became one fo the most valted careers in cinema.

Over his active years, Wilder directed films that ended-up defining entire genres. From the establish the visual language of film nior, to the most citeds comedies of the 1950's, he left behind a filmography that continues to infulence filmmakers today.
This guide is your starting point.

Film Noir

Wilder's paring cynical characters, dark suspense and biting dialouge came to define film noir in the 1940's and 1950's. His biographer would later attribute this mix of elements coming from working through the feelings of his mother, stepfather and grandmother murdered in the Holocaust.

Double Indemnity

1944; Film Noir

An insurance salesman is seduced into helping a woman murder her husband for the insurance payout. Considered the defining film noir, it set the template for every crime thriller that followed.

Sunset Boulevard **My Favorite**

1950; Film Noir

A silent film star looking to make her RETURN in the sound era. She is trapped in her own delusion and drags in a struggling screenwriter into her world. One of cinema's most haunting portrails of the rot in Hollywood.

Witness for the Prosecution

1957; Film Noir; Courtroom Drama

A cunning Courtroom thriller based on Agatha Christie's stage play. featuring one of the most satisfying twist ending (spoilers) in film history. Now, now, who are we to spoil it here?

Comedy

Wilder's comedies are endlessly quoteable, and often hide dark elements around the laughs. These films show that Wilder was a deft with comedy as he was with dramas.

Some Like It Hot

1959; Comedy

Two musicians witness a mob killing and they end up on the run dressed as women in an all-female band. Often cited as one of the best comedys ever made. Featuring Marilyn Monroe.

The Apartment

**Meh, pretty bad images of women**

1960; Comedy; Drama

A lonely office worker rents out his apartment for this scumbag company higher-ups to have affairs, only to call for one of the woman involved. It won best picture and best director.

Sabrina

1954; Romantic Comedy

The chauffeur's daughter returns from Paris a sophisticated woman, catching the attention of two weathy brothers. A charming comedy staring Audrey Hupburn.

Drama

When Wilder turned to stright drama, the results are some of the most daring films of the Studio Era. These films tackled real subjects Hollywood rearely touched.

The Lost Weekend

1945; Drama

A brutally honest portrait of an alcoholic writer's descent over a single weekend. Groundbreaking in its honesty, it won four Academy Awards including Best Picture.

Stalag 17

1953; Drama; Dark Comedy

Set in a German POW camp during WWII, this film follows prisoners who suspect one of their own is an informant. A tense, and darkly funny human story.

Ace in the Hole

1951; Drama

A cynical journalist exploits a man trapped in a cave to revive his failing career. One of Wilder's most bitter films.
